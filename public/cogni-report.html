CogniMax Research Report

Skill-Conditioned Exploitative Chess Search  ·  1,600 games, 8 skill bands  ·  June 2026

CogniMax is a chess engine that predicts how a human player at a given skill level is likely to respond, then picks moves that exploit those tendencies rather than assuming the opponent plays perfectly. Tested across 1,600 games at skill levels Elo 600 to 2000, it won games significantly faster than standard Stockfish at 6 of 8 skill bands. The advantage was largest against the strongest opponents, not the weakest. Two bands need more data to be conclusive. The main open question is whether the advantage comes from the human-behavior model specifically, or from the different search strategy alone.

Background

Standard chess engines like Stockfish assume the opponent always plays the best possible move. This means they never set traps, because a perfect opponent would never fall for one.

CogniMax takes a different approach. At each move, it asks a human-behavior model called maia3 (a neural network trained on 600 million real Lichess games) what a player at the target skill level is actually likely to play. It then picks the move with the highest expected payoff against that specific human prediction - even if that move looks weaker to a conventional evaluator. This lets it set traps that real players at that level are statistically likely to fall into.

The comparison is fair: both CogniMax and Stockfish are given the same compute budget. The only difference is the decision strategy - human-prediction vs. perfect-opponent assumption.

Hypotheses

Three predictions were made before the experiments began and tested against real results.

H1 - Speed
CogniMax wins in fewer moves. The advantage shrinks against stronger opponents, who are harder to exploit.
True, but shape was opposite
H2 - Trap Frequency
The engine sets fewer traps against stronger opponents, who are more likely to see through them.
Not true
H3 - Prediction Accuracy
When the engine sets a trap, its confidence that the opponent will fall for it should match how often they actually do.
Confirmed

Why "Moves to Win" Instead of Win Rate

The original plan was to compare win rates. This turned out to be useless: both engines are so much stronger than their opponents that they win nearly every game. Win rate cannot separate them.

Why this matters

The real question is not whether an engine wins, but how quickly. "Moves to win" stays meaningful even when both engines win 99% of games.

Why the advantage grows with opponent strength

At Elo 600, beginners blunder against almost any move. Stockfish already converts these games quickly - so there is little room for CogniMax to add speed on top.

At higher Elo, players only make mistakes under specific types of pressure. CogniMax steers every move toward the positions that this specific player is most likely to mishandle. Stockfish optimises for positions that are hard for a perfect player, which is a completely different target.

A supporting factor: maia3's ability to predict opponent moves improves at higher skill levels, because skilled players make more consistent and concentrated choices. Better predictions lead to better exploitation.

maia3 predicts higher-Elo players more accurately

Figure 4. "Coverage" = the share of actual opponent moves that the engine considered as possibilities. At Elo 600, 1 in 4 moves were never on the engine's radar. At Elo 2000, only 1 in 10. Higher coverage means better exploitation of opponent tendencies.

H2 - Trap frequency (not confirmed)

Finding

Trap frequency stayed flat across all skill bands. The prediction that it would decrease against stronger players was wrong.

A "trap played" requires two conditions at once: the move must be objectively weaker by a defined margin, AND its expected value against this specific opponent must be higher. Both conditions being true simultaneously is uncommon. Most of CogniMax's advantage comes from ordinary moves chosen to maximise expected value - not from set traps.

Trap rate is flat - does not decrease at higher skill levels

Figure 5. Trap candidates considered (grey) and traps played (red) per 100 decisions. Both are flat. About 1 in 8 candidates are played; the rest are rejected because the engine predicts the opponent would not fall for them.

H3 - Prediction accuracy (confirmed)

Finding

The engine demands higher confidence before setting a trap against weaker players, and accepts lower confidence against stronger ones. Predictions match reality within 2%.

At Elo 600, traps are only played when the engine predicts a roughly 78% chance the opponent blunders. At Elo 1800-2000, it accepts around 63%. This is not a flaw - weaker players are more unpredictable, so the engine is appropriately more cautious. The trend is statistically reliable (r = -0.19, p = 0.0003 on 363 trap events).

Engine requires higher confidence to set a trap against weaker players

Figure 6. Minimum confidence required before playing a trap, by skill band. Values are percentages: at 78% the engine needs near-certainty; at 63% it accepts moderate confidence. Declining trend is statistically reliable.

Standard accuracy metrics do not apply here

CogniMax deliberately plays objectively weaker moves when they create better expected outcomes against the specific opponent. By standard chess metrics (centipawn loss, engine accuracy), this looks like lower accuracy - which is expected, because CogniMax is optimising a different objective than those metrics measure.

Technical note

The engine accuracy column in the dataset returns 0% for every single game due to a technical bug (shallow evaluation used instead of deep analysis). It must be ignored entirely. The correct metric is opponent error rate, shown in Figure 7.

How CogniMax Wins Faster Without Always Playing Traps

The mechanism

On every single move, CogniMax steers toward positions where the specific opponent is most likely to make a mistake - not just during formal trap moments. This continuous pressure is what produces faster wins, not the occasional discrete trap.

Two things happen on every decision

Expected value steering. When selecting any move - safe or not - CogniMax asks: "Given what this specific opponent is likely to do next, which move leads to the best outcome?" Even ordinary non-trap moves are chosen based on this criterion. Stockfish asks a different question: "What is the objectively best position against a perfect opponent?" Against a real human, these two questions have different answers, and CogniMax follows the one that matters.

Tie-break preference. When two moves have nearly the same expected value, CogniMax always picks the one where the opponent's predicted blunder probability is highest. This preference applies on every decision, regardless of whether a formal trap is triggered. Over 25 to 35 moves per game, these small preferences accumulate into a systematically more error-prone game trajectory for the opponent.

Why sacrificing objective quality can be worth it

A move that costs 0.02 win-expectation objectively, but raises the opponent's error probability from 15% to 40%, has a net expected-value gain of roughly +0.25 against this opponent. Standard engines never make this trade. CogniMax makes it wherever the numbers favour it - which is most of the time at higher Elo.

This is also why conventional "engine accuracy" metrics look similar for both engines. CogniMax deliberately plays some objectively weaker moves. By standard chess metrics that penalise any deviation from the engine's best move, this looks like lower accuracy. But CogniMax is optimising a different objective - one that is directly connected to the results in Figures 7 and 8.

The risk dial (lambda)

CogniMax has a parameter called lambda that controls how willing it is to take risky traps. Think of it as a confidence dial - negative values require near-certainty, positive values accept long shots.

Limitations and Open Questions

Critical concern - circular test: The same model (maia3) is used both to predict what the opponent will do and to simulate the opponent in every game. When you know your opponent's exact strategy, any expectimax engine is mathematically guaranteed to outperform one that does not. The current results therefore partially measure a structural guarantee rather than proving the human-behavior modeling specifically is useful. The upcoming experiments test the engine against opponents that are not the same model it uses for predictions.

Elo 600 and 1000 need more data

At Elo 600, CogniMax was 2.5 moves faster but the result is not statistically reliable. To confirm it, we would need ~460 unpaired games, or ~80 paired games (where both engines play the same openings, removing a lot of noise). The paired design is planned for Run 5.

Elo 600 and 1000 show the right direction but are not yet confirmed

Figure 12. Moves saved per game. Dark red = statistically confirmed. Light red = correct direction but not enough data. Elo 600 and 1000 will be retested with a paired design in Run 5.

The advantage at Elo 2000 may keep growing above it

The effect at Elo 2000 (saving 13.5 moves, effect size 0.59) is the largest in the dataset with no sign of levelling off. It is unknown whether the trend continues, plateaus, or reverses above Elo 2000. Run 7 will test this, pending a calibration check of maia3's prediction accuracy at those skill levels.

Run 5 - Retest Elo 600 and 1000

X0 - Test against a randomly-blundering opponent

Replace maia3 (the simulated human) with Stockfish playing at reduced skill using a built-in "Skill Level" setting. This opponent makes random mistakes rather than human-pattern ones. CogniMax still uses maia3 predictions. 80 paired games at Elo 1400, 1600, 1800, 2000.

First circularity break. If the advantage shrinks compared to maia3 games, it means the human-behavior model is specifically useful (not just the search structure). ~5 hours Colab.

3

X1 - Ablation: how much of the advantage comes from the human model?

Five engine versions compared at Elo 600, 1000, 1600, 2000 (80 pairs each): (a) Stockfish control, (b) CogniMax with maia3 (current), (c) CogniMax with uniform probability over all legal moves (expectimax structure, no human model at all), (d) CogniMax using Stockfish's own probability estimates, (e) CogniMax without the preference for error-inducing moves.

If version (c) performs as well as (b), the advantage comes from the search structure, not the human model. If (b) is faster, the human model adds real value beyond structure alone. This is the most important experiment. ~18 hours Colab.

4

X6 - Test against Leela Chess Zero (Lc0)

Replace maia3 with Lc0 (trained entirely on self-play, no human games, different architecture) at low strength. CogniMax still uses maia3 predictions. 80 paired games x 4 skill bands. Lc0 is already installed.

If CogniMax still wins faster, the advantage generalises to non-maia3 opponents. ~12 hours Colab.

5

X7 - Validate against real human Lichess games

At positions where CogniMax and Stockfish recommend different moves, find real Lichess games where each move was played, and check how often the real human opponent made a mistake within 5 moves. Uses logistic regression to control for skill level, time, and position type.

Gold-standard test: real human behaviour, no simulation, no circularity. About 1 day of setup, ~3 hours Colab.

Run 6 - Adaptive risk schedule

Lambda = -16 at Elo 600 rising to +12 at Elo 2000. 100 paired games per band, all 8 bands. Based on Run 4 internal test, which showed lambda = -16 gave the most reliable results.

Tests whether adapting risk tolerance to skill level flattens or improves the advantage curve. ~10 hours Colab.

Run 8 - Tactically rich opening positions
